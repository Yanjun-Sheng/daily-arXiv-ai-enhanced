### [1] [The diagnostic temperature discrepancy as evidence for non-Maxwellian coronal electrons](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.10040)
*Victor Edmonds*

Main category: astro-ph.SR

TL;DR: Radio and hydrostatic diagnostics of quiet solar corona yield systematically different electron temperatures (0.6 MK vs 1.5 MK), suggesting non-Maxwellian electron distributions with kappa ~ 2-3 rather than instrumental error or scattering effects.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To resolve the persistent discrepancy between two independent electron temperature diagnostics in the quiet solar corona: radio brightness temperatures (~0.6 MK) versus hydrostatic scale-height modeling (~1.5 MK), which disagree by a factor of 2.4±0.3 across eight years of solar cycle data.

Method: Analyzed eight-year dataset of Nancay Radioheliograph radio brightness temperatures and hydrostatic scale-height modeling of the same plasma. Considered alternative explanations (turbulent scattering), then proposed non-Maxwellian electron velocity distributions (kappa distributions) as explanation. Derived theoretical ratio R = kappa/(kappa - 3/2) from kappa distribution formalism.

Result: The temperature discrepancy (R = 2.4±0.3) persists across solar cycle despite expected turbulence variations, ruling out scattering effects. The observed ratio implies kappa ~ 2-3, consistent with spectroscopic measurements in active regions but in tension with theoretical predictions of kappa ~ 10-25. Predicts collapsed ratio (R ≤ 1.5) in Active Region cores due to higher collisionality.

Conclusion: The persistent temperature discrepancy reflects non-Maxwellian electron velocity distributions in the quiet solar corona. Radio bremsstrahlung samples the distribution core while ionization rates and scale heights are dominated by suprathermal tails. This invalidates application of standard fluid transport equations (Spitzer-Harm conductivity) to such plasmas, creating an open problem for heat flux calculations.

Abstract: Two independent electron temperature diagnostics applied to the quiet solar corona yield systematically different results. Radio brightness temperatures from the Nancay Radioheliograph indicate T_e ~ 0.6 MK, while hydrostatic scale-height modeling of the same plasma requires T_e ~ 1.5 MK (Mercier & Chambe 2015). Both diagnostics probe electrons; they disagree by a factor of R = 2.4 +/- 0.3. This discrepancy persists across an eight-year dataset spanning solar minimum and is consistent with LOFAR observations at lower frequencies (Vocks et al. 2018). We consider the propagation alternative (turbulent scattering of radio emission), which operates in the correct direction to suppress the apparent brightness temperature, but the ratio R is invariant over the solar cycle despite expected variations in turbulence levels. We propose that the residual, cycle-invariant discrepancy reflects non-Maxwellian electron velocity distributions. Radio bremsstrahlung samples the distribution core, while ionization rates and scale heights are dominated by the suprathermal tail. For kappa distributions, the predicted ratio is kappa/(kappa - 3/2); the observed R = 2.4 implies kappa ~ 2-3. This is consistent with spectroscopic measurements in active regions but in tension with perturbative theoretical predictions of kappa ~ 10-25. We make falsifiable predictions: Active Region cores should show a collapsed ratio (R <= 1.5) as collisionality restores thermal equilibrium. Applying fluid transport equations (Spitzer-Harm conductivity) to plasmas with kappa ~ 2-3 is physically invalid, but we do not compute the resulting heat flux, which remains an open problem.
